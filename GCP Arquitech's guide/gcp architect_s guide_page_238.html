Correlated Failures and Failure due to Overload

Correlated failures occur when a single event causes a chain reaction of failures. For example, if a zone or region is lost, all resources in it will inevitably fail. This is known as a failure domain. To identify potential failure domains during the design process, divide business logic into services based on failure domains. This approach considers resiliency for entire failure domains rather than individual components.

Dividing business logic into services based on failure domains makes it easier to look at the problem from a high-level perspective, such as identifying failure domains at the zone or regional level. The goal is to separate and isolate the risk of correlated failures while designing independent but collaborative services.

Failure due to Overload

When a system is overloaded, it will exhibit nonlinear behavior, such as local applications hanging, crashing, disks thrashing, or stopping altogether. Overloading occurs for several reasons, but a common cause is the relationship between failure due to loss and failure due to overload.

For instance, if a system goes offline due to the loss of a downstream resource, it becomes unavailable to upstream systems that depend on it. When the system comes back online, the queued backlog of work must be processed before new work can be handled. This may overwhelm the system, causing it to transition from being unavailable due to resource loss to being unavailable due to overload.

Common behaviors of failure due to overload include running out of memory, high CPU usage, or disk thrashing. Regardless of the symptoms, unavailability will create a chain reaction of effects, leading to failures in dependent services. To identify potential points of overload, take an overall look at the networking service architecture.

This is because once there's overload on a resource, such as an overloaded frontend, a large list of cascading failures will occur. Troubleshooting can be challenging when dealing with multiple independent alarms being triggered. Therefore, it's essential to identify in the design process what are the critical first things that can happen, which indicate an overload.
